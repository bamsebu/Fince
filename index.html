This paper sets out to investigate the usefulness of different approaches in EC. By referring to examples in literature, the paper is limited to gain insights in two approaches or lenses; democracy and communication. The paper is designed to determine how democracy and communication based research contributes to investigation in EC. The first section describes and contrasts theories, concepts and methodologies, while the final section highlights weaknesses and strengths in the approaches. After the Second World War there was a rising wave of scholars who researched how totalitarian regimes came to dominate over established democratic systems, and how it could be refrained from happening again (Hansen, von Essen & Sriskandarajah 2016, pp. 114-116). In this context, works of critical social theorist Jürgen Habermas became inevitable . As we shall see, Habermas’ work, such as the public sphere theory, is applicable to both the communication and democracy approach. The public sphere can best be described “as a network for communicating information and points of view […]” (Habermas 1996, p. 360). In research within the two approaches and EC, the theory is commonly used along with application of deliberative democracy. This form of democracy encompasses that people’s views should be represented equally and under conditions in which one can think and come to a considered judgement (Fishkin 2009, p. 13). The deliberative democracy is generally understood to be about participating, as well as how you participate. Within the two approaches, it is especially difficult to bypass conceptualization of the deliberative democracy in the democracy approach. Furthermore, according to Hansen et al. (2016), due to neoliberal ideology’s dominance in problem management, the deliberative democracy is being eroded. Neoliberal ideology refers to the logic of letting the market steer society, even at the expense of democracy.
